Module 1

Three Shifts at Once

AI has been around for decades. Your spam filter is AI. Netflix recommendations are AI. So why does right now feel different?

Because three things changed in a short window. Any one alone would matter. All three together is why this moment is different from the last ten years of "AI is coming."

1 The pace is relentless

A new model, a new feature, a new capability — every week. Not annual product cycles. Not quarterly updates. Weekly. The gap between "cutting edge research" and "button in your software" has collapsed from years to months, sometimes weeks. By the time you've finished evaluating one tool, three more have launched.

This is why it feels impossible to keep up. It's not a knowledge problem — it's a speed problem. And it's not slowing down. Roughly 95% of companies haven't seen real financial impact from AI yet. Most businesses are where you are right now — aware, interested, not sure where to start. That's normal.

79% of enterprises say AI adoption is getting harder, not easier. The window doesn't stay open just because most people haven't climbed through it yet.

2 The cost collapsed — then the bills arrived

The cost of a single AI query dropped over 99% between 2023 and today. That's why every piece of software you use suddenly has an AI button — for the vendor, adding it cost almost nothing. Most of these features are already included in what you pay.

But cheap per query doesn't mean cheap at scale. Uber gave their engineers access to AI coding tools and burned through their entire 2026 AI budget by April — four months in. Nvidia's own VP said publicly that his team's compute costs now exceed what he pays the people. Getting started is nearly free. Scaling without understanding what you're scaling is where it gets expensive.

3 The capability jumped

Until recently, AI was a search engine with better grammar. You asked, it answered, you did the work. That's not where it is now. AI agents log into your actual business tools and take action. Sending follow-up emails. Updating records. Generating reports from live data.

And it's not just the boring work. AI writes production code. It reads contracts and spots risk clauses. It handles nuanced customer conversations, not scripted FAQ responses. The line between "AI work" and "human work" is moving — not just at the admin end, through the middle and into the complex, skilled, well-paid end.

Block's AI resolves customer issues end to end without a human involved. Seventy percent of Uber's code now originates from AI.
