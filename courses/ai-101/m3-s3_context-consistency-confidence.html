Module 3

Context, Consistency, and Confidence

Three things trip people up once they start relying on AI. All three make sense once you know how next-token prediction works.

1 The context window

Every conversation with AI has a size limit. Your messages, the AI's replies, any documents you've pasted in, the system's own instructions — all of it sits in a single window of context.

When it's full, the oldest parts get quietly dropped. The AI doesn't flag it. It just stops having access to what you said earlier.

This is why AI "forgets." Not because it's broken. Because your earlier messages fell outside the window. A 50-page report, a few rounds of back-and-forth, and the system's built-in instructions eat through it faster than you'd expect.

Practical fix: When a conversation starts going sideways, don't keep arguing with it. Start a new chat and paste in the key points you need it to hold onto. That resets the window with only what matters.

AI doesn't have a memory. It has a window. Everything inside exists. Everything outside doesn't.

2 Why AI is inconsistent

Ask AI "Give me three marketing ideas for a small business" right now. Then ask it again. Different ideas. Different phrasing. Maybe a completely different angle. This isn't a bug.

The model picks from the highest-probability tokens, but it doesn't always pick the single top one. There's a setting called temperature that controls how much variety gets introduced.

When you use AI through an API or build automations, temperature is one of the first things you control. Low for consistency — standard client emails, data extraction. Higher for creative work — brainstorming, drafting, ideation.

3 AI invents things. And doesn't know it.

The model isn't looking things up. It's not searching a database of facts. It's predicting what a correct-sounding response looks like based on patterns.

Most of the time, the patterns line up with reality. "The capital of France is Paris" — that pattern is so strong it's essentially always right.

But ask for something specific — a legal clause, a citation, a statistic — and the model will produce whatever token sequence looks most like a correct answer. Confidently. With no indication it just made the whole thing up.

The technical term is hallucination. The practical reality: AI is a pattern-completion machine, not a fact-retrieval system.

1
Never trust AI on specific facts, numbers, names, or dates without checking. Use it to draft, structure, and accelerate — then verify anything that matters.
2
The more confidently AI states something obscure, the more suspicious you should be. Common knowledge is reliable. Niche claims are where fabrication gets creative.

Hallucinations, bias, and the harder risks are covered later. For now: every response is a prediction, not a retrieval. Treat it accordingly.
