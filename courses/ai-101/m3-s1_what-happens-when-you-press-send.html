Module 3

What Happens When You Press Send

You type "Write me a follow-up email for a client who hasn't replied in two weeks" into ChatGPT. Three seconds later, a perfectly structured email appears. Knowing what happened in those three seconds changes how you use every AI tool from here on.

Your words become tokens

AI doesn't read words the way you do. The first thing it does is break your message into chunks called tokens — somewhere between letters and whole words. "Don't" becomes two tokens. "The" is one. "Entrepreneurial" might be three.

One token is roughly three-quarters of a word in English. A 1,000-word document is about 1,300 tokens. This matters because tokens are how AI measures everything — the length of your question, the length of the answer, and what you get charged for. When a response cuts off mid-sentence, it hit a token limit.

Try this
Ask ChatGPT or Claude "How many tokens is this message?" with a short sentence, then a long paragraph. You'll see the difference immediately.

The model predicts the next token. One at a time.

The AI doesn't read your prompt and plan a response. No outline, no draft, no "I know what email this person needs." It does one thing: looks at all the tokens so far and calculates which token is most likely to come next. Not which is correct. Not which is best. Which is most probable, based on patterns from training.

Every token in its vocabulary — tens of thousands of them — gets a probability score. "Subject" might score high (emails often start there). "Dear" scores high too. "Banana" scores near zero. It picks from the top. Now it has your original prompt plus "Subject". Same question: what's most likely next? ":" scores high. Picked.

Every word you've ever received from an AI tool was chosen this way. Token by token. Each one the highest-probability next piece given everything before it.

It sees everything behind it. Nothing ahead.

Each time the model picks a token, it can see your full prompt plus every token of the response it's already written — all at once, all weighed against each other simultaneously. That's the engineering breakthrough that made modern AI work. Older AI read left to right and lost track of the start by the time it reached the end. Current models hold the full picture at every step.

But the model hasn't planned what comes next. When it's writing token 50, token 51 doesn't exist yet. The email you're reading was never drafted as a whole — it emerged one prediction at a time, each informed by everything before it, with no blueprint for what follows.

Watch the text appear in ChatGPT or Claude next time you use it. That's not a typing animation. Each word lands because the model just decided it was the most probable next token.

Why this works far better than autocomplete

"Predicting the next word" sounds like it should produce garbage. Your phone does the same thing and the results are usually useless. Three things make AI different:

1
Training at scale
These models processed billions of pages of text. At that volume, the patterns go far beyond word associations. The model learned how professional emails are structured, how arguments build, how a follow-up differs from a cold outreach. The patterns run deep enough that prediction produces genuinely useful output — even though no "understanding" is happening underneath.
2
Human feedback
After training, humans rated thousands of AI responses — this one's helpful, this one's not, this one's harmful — and the model was tuned to favour the kind of output people actually wanted. That's why it answers your question instead of continuing your text like a paragraph generator.
3
Reasoning (in newer models)
The latest versions of Claude, ChatGPT, and Gemini can generate a chain of internal reasoning tokens before the response you see — working through the problem step by step. Still next-token prediction, but now the model is predicting against its own working-out, not just your prompt. Better scratch paper, better output.

What this means for you

Every AI tool you use runs on this same mechanic. You're not talking to something that knows things. You're working with something that is extraordinarily good at predicting what a helpful response looks like — based on patterns, feedback, and context.

Most of the time, that prediction is right. When it's not, it's wrong with total confidence. Understanding this single fact will save you from the two biggest mistakes people make with AI: trusting it completely, or dismissing it entirely.
