Module 1

AI Didn't Start with ChatGPT

The media treats late 2022 as the moment AI arrived. It wasn't — it was the moment most people noticed. This section covers how we got here, through five distinct eras. Each one builds on the last.

If the history isn't your thing, skip ahead. You won't miss anything you need for the rest of the course.

Era 1
Concept Era
1950s - 1980s
The dream is established. Machines that think remain science fiction — but the theoretical framework is laid.

What it could do: Follow logical rules. Play chess (badly). Solve maths problems.

What it couldn't: Learn from experience. Handle anything it wasn't explicitly programmed for.

Business impact: Almost none. This was research labs and universities. But it laid the groundwork for everything that followed.

Why it matters now
You won't see Concept Era AI in your business today. But vendors sometimes use language from this era — "intelligent systems," "smart automation" — to make basic rule-following sound more impressive than it is.
Era 2
Rules Era
1980s - 1990s
Expert systems arrive. AI follows rigid, human-written rules — fast and consistent, but brittle.

What it could do: Apply if/then logic at scale. "If the transaction is over $10,000 and from a new account, flag it."

What it couldn't: Handle ambiguity. Adapt when the rules didn't cover a situation. Learn from mistakes.

  • Fraud detection based on fixed thresholds
  • Credit scoring with predefined criteria
  • Manufacturing quality checks
Still running today
You almost certainly use Rules Era AI without calling it AI. Your email filters, your accounting software's auto-categorisation, your HR system's compliance flags — these are all if/then rules applied at speed.
Era 3
Pattern Era
2000s - 2010s
Machine Learning arrives. Instead of following rules, AI finds its own patterns in data.

What it could do: Find patterns in data that humans couldn't see. Recognise faces. Predict which customers would leave. Recommend products.

What it couldn't: Explain why it found a pattern. Generate anything new. Understand context or language naturally.

  • Spam filters — your inbox learned to recognise junk by analysing millions of examples
  • Recommendations — Netflix and Amazon predicted what you'd want next
  • Fraud detection — banks flagged transactions that didn't "look right" against your typical spending
Business impact
When someone says "our AI learns from your data," they're usually talking about Pattern Era technology. Powerful — but fundamentally about recognition and prediction, not creation or action.
Era 4
Generation Era
2020 - 2024
Large Language Models change the interface. For the first time, AI can create — and anyone can use it.

This is ChatGPT, Claude, Midjourney, Copilot. For the first time, AI could produce output that looked like it came from a human. Draft an email. Write a blog post. Summarise a contract.

But — and this is critical — Generation Era AI is still a tool you use. You ask, it generates, you check, you do something with the output. The human is still in the loop for every action.

Where most people are
Most people's experience of AI right now is Generation Era. And most of what's being sold to businesses is Generation Era with a nice interface on top.
Era 5 — Now
Action Era
2024 - Present
The agentic shift. AI moves from chatbot to agent — from something you talk to, to something that does work for you.

AI agents log into your actual business tools and take action. Sending follow-up emails. Updating records. Generating reports from live data.

The gap between Era 4 and Era 5 is the gap between "AI helped me write this" and "AI did this while I was doing something else." That's a fundamentally different relationship with technology.

  • Logs into your accounting software to identify late payments
  • Checks email history to see if the client has already responded
  • Drafts a personalised follow-up based on that history
  • Sends the email — or flags it for approval if the amount exceeds a threshold
Business impact
AI transitions from creative assistant to functional team member. It completes multi-step business workflows autonomously — not just generates words.

Why vendors blur the lines

A tool that applies fixed rules will call itself "AI-powered." A tool that does basic pattern matching will claim to be "intelligent." A tool that generates text will say it "automates your workflow" — even though you still have to copy, paste, and send.

This isn't always dishonest. But it is often imprecise. And if you're making buying decisions based on imprecise claims, you'll overpay for capability you don't get.

Three questions to cut through it:

"Does it learn, or does it follow rules I set?"
If it only does what you configure, it's Era 2. Useful, but not AI in the way most people mean it now.
"Does it create things, or does it do things?"
If it generates a draft and hands it to you, it's Era 4. If it takes the draft, sends it, and logs the result without your involvement, it's Era 5. The difference in value — and risk — is significant.
"What happens when it gets something wrong?"
Era 3 and 4 tools are wrong sometimes and you catch it because you're in the loop. Era 5 tools are wrong sometimes and nobody catches it unless you've built in checks. That's the trade-off of autonomy.
